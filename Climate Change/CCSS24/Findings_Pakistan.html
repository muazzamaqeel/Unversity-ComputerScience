COUNTRY OVERVIEW

The Islamic Republic of Pakistan is a South Asian country located at the crossroads of Central and Western Asia. It shares borders with India to the east, Afghanistan and Iran to the west, and China to the north. To the south, it has a coastline along the Arabian Sea. Pakistan's diverse landscape includes mountain ranges such as the Himalayas and the Karakoram, vast deserts like the Thar, fertile plains in the Punjab, and a significant portion of the Indus River basin. The country has a population of approximately 240 million (2024), making it the fifth most populous country in the world, with a high population density in urban areas.

Pakistan’s economy is primarily based on agriculture, industry, and services. Agriculture, which includes major crops like wheat, rice, and cotton, contributes 19% to the GDP and employs about 42% of the labor force. The industrial sector, which includes textiles, cement, and food processing, contributes around 20% to the GDP. The services sector is the largest contributor, making up about 61% of the GDP. Pakistan is classified as a lower-middle-income country, with a GDP per capita of $1,632.70 in 2023, showing modest growth from $1,530.20 in 2022. The economy faces challenges such as political instability, security concerns, and natural disasters, which impact growth and development.

Climate Baseline

Pakistan’s topography ranges from the towering peaks of the northern mountains to the coastal areas in the south. The country experiences a variety of climates due to its diverse geography. The northern regions have a harsh alpine climate with severe winters, while the southern regions near the Arabian Sea experience a more tropical climate. The central regions, including the Indus River plains, have a semi-arid to arid climate. Pakistan's climate can be broadly categorized into four seasons: a cool, dry winter from December to February; a hot, dry spring from March to May; the summer rainy season, or monsoon, from June to September; and the retreating monsoon period of October and November.

Average temperatures in Pakistan vary widely depending on the region and season. In the summer, temperatures can reach 50°C in the southern plains, while the northern mountainous areas remain much cooler. Winter temperatures can drop below freezing in the north, while the southern regions stay relatively mild. Precipitation is highly seasonal and varies significantly across the country. The monsoon season brings heavy rainfall to the eastern and northern regions, while the western areas remain dry. Average annual precipitation ranges from less than 250 millimeters (mm) in the arid regions to over 1,500 mm in the northern areas.

Key Impacts of Climate Change in Pakistan

Temperature Rise

Pakistan has experienced a significant rise in average temperatures over the past few decades, consistent with global warming trends. Between 1951 and 2023, the average temperature in Pakistan increased by approximately 0.26°C per decade. The warming trend is more pronounced in the northern and central regions, particularly during the summer months. This has resulted in longer and more intense heatwaves, which impact human health, agriculture, and water resources. Projections indicate that temperatures will continue to rise, leading to more extreme weather events and further stressing Pakistan’s natural and economic systems.

Water Resources

Climate change poses a severe threat to Pakistan’s water resources, which are vital for agriculture, industry, and domestic use. The country relies heavily on the Indus River system, fed by glaciers in the Himalayas and the Karakoram. These glaciers are retreating due to rising temperatures, reducing the flow of rivers and impacting water availability. Seasonal variations in river flows, coupled with increased demand from a growing population, exacerbate water stress. Flooding during the monsoon season and droughts during dry periods further strain water resources. Effective water management is crucial to address these challenges and ensure sustainable water use.

Agriculture

Pakistan’s agricultural sector is highly vulnerable to climate variability and change. Changes in precipitation patterns, increased frequency of extreme weather events, and rising temperatures threaten crop yields and livestock. Wheat, rice, and cotton, which are critical to Pakistan’s economy, are particularly affected. Projections suggest that wheat yields could decline by up to 30% by 2050 without adaptation measures. Adaptation strategies include introducing drought-resistant crop varieties, improving irrigation practices, and adopting climate-smart agricultural techniques. These measures are essential for maintaining agricultural productivity and food security in the face of climate change.

Desertification and Land Degradation

Desertification and land degradation are significant challenges in Pakistan, exacerbated by climate change and unsustainable land management practices. Overgrazing, deforestation, and improper agricultural practices contribute to soil erosion and loss of fertile land, particularly in the arid and semi-arid regions. Increased temperatures and decreased precipitation worsen soil salinity and desertification risks. Dust storms, which are common in the Thar Desert and other arid areas, further degrade land and negatively impact agriculture and living conditions. Sustainable land management practices are necessary to combat these issues and restore degraded lands.

Biodiversity

Climate change poses a threat to Pakistan’s rich biodiversity, affecting ecosystems and wildlife habitats. Changes in temperature and precipitation patterns disrupt the natural habitats of many species, leading to shifts in their distribution and behavior. Species unable to adapt to these changes face increased extinction risks. Wetlands, forests, and coastal ecosystems are particularly vulnerable. Conservation efforts, including protected areas and wildlife corridors, are critical to preserving biodiversity and maintaining ecological balance.

Mitigation and Adaptation Efforts

Renewable Energy

Pakistan is investing in renewable energy sources to reduce its reliance on fossil fuels and decrease greenhouse gas emissions. The government aims to increase the share of renewables, such as wind, solar, and hydropower, in the energy mix. Large-scale solar projects in the Thar Desert and wind farms in Sindh and Balochistan are part of these efforts. Promoting renewable energy is crucial for sustainable development and combating climate change.

Sustainable Land Management

Efforts to promote sustainable land management practices are underway to combat desertification and improve soil health. These include afforestation, reforestation, and conservation agriculture techniques. Projects focusing on planting drought-resistant trees, adopting soil conservation methods, and restoring degraded lands are being implemented. These initiatives help to prevent further land degradation and support sustainable agriculture.

Water Management

Improved water management strategies are critical to addressing the challenges posed by climate change. Efficient irrigation systems, water-saving technologies, and integrated water resource management plans are essential components of these efforts. The government is implementing policies to enhance water use efficiency, promote sustainable water practices, and modernize irrigation infrastructure. Managing transboundary water resources through regional cooperation is also a priority to ensure water security.

Climate Policy

Pakistan has developed national strategies and action plans to address climate change, demonstrating its commitment to international agreements like the Paris Agreement. These policies aim to reduce carbon emissions and enhance climate resilience through comprehensive measures. Key actions include improving energy efficiency, promoting renewable energy, and implementing climate-adaptive agricultural practices. The government is also focused on fostering regional cooperation to manage shared water resources effectively and mitigate climate impacts.
